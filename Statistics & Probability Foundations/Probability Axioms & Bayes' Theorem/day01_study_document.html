Sample Spaces, Events & The Three Axioms

Before we can talk about probability, we need precise language. The concepts here are simple — but the precision matters enormously. Imprecise probability language is where most analysts go wrong.

1.1 The Sample Space (Ω)

A sample space (written Ω, "omega") is the set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment. Every probability question begins by defining this space clearly.

ExperimentSample Space ΩSize
A single coin flip{Heads, Tails}2
A transaction is processed{fraud, legitimate}2
A transaction amount[0, ∞) — any positive real numberInfinite
Card type used{Visa, Mastercard, Amex, Discover, Other}5
Number of disputes in a day{0, 1, 2, 3, ...}Countably infinite
Intuition

Sample space = the universe of what could happen

When you build a fraud model, your implicit sample space is every transaction that could ever occur. When you run a report on yesterday's chargebacks, your sample space is every transaction processed that day. Being explicit about the sample space prevents you from making probability statements that don't make sense.

1.2 Events

An event is any subset of the sample space — a collection of outcomes you care about. Events are usually written as capital letters: A, B, C.

  • Elementary event: A single outcome. E.g., {fraud}
  • Compound event: Multiple outcomes. E.g., {Visa, Mastercard} = "card is a major network"
  • Impossible event (∅): An event that can never happen. P(∅) = 0
  • Certain event (Ω): Something that always happens. P(Ω) = 1
Your Domain · Payments

Payment events you work with every day

Event A = "transaction is fraudulent" · Event B = "transaction amount > $500" · Event C = "card used is not present" · Event D = "transaction declined" · Your job as a risk analyst is to reason about how these events relate — that's exactly what probability formalizes.

1.3 The Three Axioms of Probability (Kolmogorov)

All of probability theory rests on just three axioms, stated by Andrey Kolmogorov in 1933. Everything else — Bayes' theorem, the Central Limit Theorem, your fraud model's AUC — is derived from these three rules.

The Three Axioms of Probability

Everything in statistics is a consequence of these three rules. Memorize them. Understand why each is necessary.

Common Mistake

Probabilities must sum to 1 — always check this

If someone hands you a fraud model that says P(fraud) = 0.03 and P(legitimate) = 0.94, something is wrong — those add to 0.97, not 1. Either there's a missing category (disputes? pending?) or there's a rounding error. Always verify your probability space is complete. This is Axiom 2 in action.

Section 2 · 10:00 AM – 10:45 AM

Set Operations: Union, Intersection, Complement

Events can be combined. These three operations — plus one critical formula — are how we reason about multiple events happening together or separately.

Set Operations Visualized — Payment Events

The General Addition Rule P(A∪B) = P(A)+P(B)−P(A∩B) prevents double-counting outcomes that belong to both A and B.

Key Formula
The General Addition Rule
P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B)

Why subtract the intersection? Because any outcome in both A and B gets counted once in P(A), once in P(B) — so it's been counted twice. We subtract one copy. This is the fix for double-counting.

Special case: If A and B are mutually exclusive (can't both happen), then P(A∩B) = 0, so P(A∪B) = P(A) + P(B). This is Axiom 3.

Worked Example 1

Payment Fraud: Computing Union Probability

In your payments database, you observe the following about transactions:

  • P(fraud) = 0.02 (2% of transactions are fraudulent)
  • P(amount > $1000) = 0.08 (8% are high-value)
  • P(fraud AND amount > $1000) = 0.012

Question: What is the probability that a transaction is either fraudulent OR high-value (or both)?

1
Identify the formula
P(fraud ∪ high_value) = P(fraud) + P(high_value) − P(fraud ∩ high_value)
2
Plug in the values
= 0.02 + 0.08 − 0.012
3
Calculate
= 0.088
Answer: 8.8% of transactions are either fraudulent or high-value (or both).

Why not just add 2% + 8% = 10%? Because the 1.2% of transactions that are BOTH fraudulent AND high-value would be counted twice. We subtract it once to correct this.

Section 3 · 10:45 AM – 11:30 AM

Conditional Probability

This is the heart of probabilistic reasoning in fraud analytics. Conditional probability lets us update our beliefs when we learn new information. Every time you see a new transaction feature, you are — whether you knew it or not — performing conditional probability reasoning.

Definition
Conditional Probability P(A | B)
P(A | B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B), provided P(B) > 0

Read as: "The probability of A, given that B has occurred." You are shrinking the sample space from Ω down to just B, then asking what fraction of B is also A.

Conditional Probability — Shrinking the Sample Space

When we condition on B (card-not-present transactions), we shrink our universe to only B. We then ask: of all CNP transactions, what fraction are fraudulent? That fraction is P(Fraud | CNP).

Your Domain · Payments

Conditional probability is your core daily tool

Every fraud score your models produce is a conditional probability: P(fraud | features). When you say "this transaction looks risky," you are computing P(fraud | card_not_present=1, new_merchant=1, amount=$2500) intuitively. Today we make that formal.

Worked Example 2

Fraud Rate by Card-Not-Present Status

Your payment system processes 1,000,000 transactions per day. You know:

  • 300,000 are card-not-present (CNP) transactions → P(CNP) = 0.30
  • 20,000 are both fraudulent AND CNP → P(fraud ∩ CNP) = 0.02
  • Total fraudulent transactions: 22,000 → P(fraud) = 0.022

Question 1: What is P(fraud | CNP)? — fraud rate among CNP transactions

Question 2: What is P(fraud | NOT CNP)? — fraud rate among card-present transactions

1
P(fraud | CNP)
P(fraud | CNP) = P(fraud ∩ CNP) / P(CNP) = 0.02 / 0.30 = 0.0667

About 6.7% of CNP transactions are fraudulent.

2
P(fraud ∩ NOT CNP)
= P(fraud) − P(fraud ∩ CNP) = 0.022 − 0.02 = 0.002
3
P(NOT CNP) = 1 − 0.30 = 0.70
P(fraud | NOT CNP) = 0.002 / 0.70 = 0.00286

About 0.29% of card-present transactions are fraudulent.

CNP transactions have a 6.67% fraud rate vs 0.29% for card-present — 23x higher risk. This is precisely why CNP is a critical feature in every fraud model.
Section 4 · After Lunch · 14:00 PM – 14:45 PM

Independence vs Mutual Exclusivity

These two concepts are frequently confused. Understanding the difference is critical — getting it wrong leads to catastrophically incorrect probability calculations.

Independence vs Mutual Exclusivity — The Critical Distinction

Independent: knowing B happened tells you nothing about A. Mutually exclusive: if A happens, B definitely didn't (and vice versa). These are fundamentally different ideas.

The Critical Formulas

Independence
Events A and B are independent if and only if:
P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)

Equivalently: P(A|B) = P(A). Knowing B occurred gives you no information about whether A occurred. The multiplication rule simplifies dramatically when events are independent.

Mutual Exclusivity
Events A and B are mutually exclusive if:
P(A ∩ B) = 0 → P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B)

They cannot both happen. But — critically — this does NOT mean they are independent! In fact, if A and B are mutually exclusive and both have P > 0, they are DEPENDENT, because knowing A happened tells you B definitely didn't.

The Classic Confusion

Mutually exclusive ≠ Independent

If a transaction is flagged as "fraud" and "not fraud" are mutually exclusive, they are also maximally dependent — knowing it's fraud tells you with certainty it's not legitimate. Independent events can occur together. Mutually exclusive events cannot. Do not confuse these two concepts — it leads to wrong probability calculations in fraud models and A/B tests.

Your Domain · Fraud Analytics

When to use which assumption in payments

Independence (often violated!): Naive fraud models sometimes assume features are independent — that P(CNP=1 AND night_transaction=1) = P(CNP=1) × P(night=1). In reality, CNP and night transactions are correlated. This assumption violation is why Naive Bayes fraud models underperform tree-based models.

Mutual exclusivity: Outcome classes (fraud/legitimate) in binary classification are mutually exclusive by definition. Transaction authorization outcomes (approved/declined) are mutually exclusive.

Section 5 · 14:45 PM – 16:00 PM — THE MOST IMPORTANT SECTION TODAY

Bayes' Theorem

This is the most important formula in all of statistics for fraud analytics. Every time you update a fraud score based on new evidence, you are applying Bayes' theorem. We will derive it from scratch, understand every term, and apply it to three real payment scenarios.

5.1 Deriving Bayes' Theorem

We need two things we already know:

  1. Conditional probability: P(A|B) = P(A∩B) / P(B), so P(A∩B) = P(A|B) × P(B)
  2. Conditional probability again: P(B|A) = P(A∩B) / P(A), so P(A∩B) = P(B|A) × P(A)

Since both right-hand sides equal P(A∩B), they equal each other:

Derivation Complete
Bayes' Theorem
P(A|B) = [ P(B|A) × P(A) ] / P(B)

That's it. It falls directly from the definition of conditional probability. No magic. Just algebra.

5.2 Understanding Every Term

Bayes' Theorem — Every Term Explained for Fraud Analytics

Bayes' theorem is a machine for updating beliefs. You start with a prior (base rate), see evidence, and produce a posterior (updated probability). This is exactly what a fraud model does, mathematically.

5.3 The Law of Total Probability (for computing the denominator)

The denominator P(B) in Bayes' theorem is often computed using the Law of Total Probability. If A₁, A₂, ..., Aₙ partition the sample space (mutually exclusive, exhaustive), then:

Law of Total Probability
P(B) = P(B|A₁)P(A₁) + P(B|A₂)P(A₂) + ... + P(B|Aₙ)P(Aₙ)

For the two-class case (fraud / legitimate): P(Features) = P(Features|Fraud)×P(Fraud) + P(Features|Legit)×P(Legit)

Worked Example 3 — The Most Important One Today

Bayes' Theorem: Updating Fraud Probability with Evidence

Your fraud system receives a transaction. Before seeing any features, the base fraud rate is P(Fraud) = 0.02 (2%). You observe: the transaction is card-not-present (CNP).

From your historical data:

  • P(CNP | Fraud) = 0.80 → 80% of fraudulent transactions are CNP
  • P(CNP | Legit) = 0.25 → 25% of legitimate transactions are CNP
  • P(Fraud) = 0.02, P(Legit) = 0.98

Question: Given that the transaction is CNP, what is the updated probability of fraud?

1
Compute the denominator P(CNP) using the Law of Total Probability
P(CNP) = P(CNP|Fraud)×P(Fraud) + P(CNP|Legit)×P(Legit)
= (0.80 × 0.02) + (0.25 × 0.98)
= 0.016 + 0.245 = 0.261
2
Apply Bayes' Theorem
P(Fraud | CNP) = P(CNP | Fraud) × P(Fraud) / P(CNP)
= (0.80 × 0.02) / 0.261
= 0.016 / 0.261 = 0.0613
Answer: P(Fraud | CNP) ≈ 6.1%. The CNP signal tripled our fraud probability from 2% to 6.1%.

The intuition: Even though 80% of fraud is CNP, most CNP transactions are still legitimate (because fraud is rare overall). Bayes' theorem forces us to account for the base rate — this is called the "base rate fallacy" when people ignore it. Many fraud analysts over-estimate fraud rates because they forget the prior.

Now try it yourself: What would P(Fraud|CNP) be if P(Fraud) = 0.10? Recalculate and observe how the posterior changes with a different prior.
